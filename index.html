A 'position' is a property in CSS that defines how the element interacts with the page. Relative, absolute, and fixed Positioning are important to understand because they can be really useful when you want to move or static-ify elements on a page. Let's start with relative positioning. If I have an image at the top of my webpage and I scroll, the image will not be lost into the scroll multiverse. That is to say that it won't disappear once you scroll down. You can also add fancy properties to place the component exactly where you want relative to its original position in the document flow. Absolute positioning is a bit different. It's literally a post-it for your computer screen. You can position it anywhere you want and it'll just stay there. BUT it is dependent on the placement of its closest ancestor. It's kind of like an anchor on a ship. Wherever it goes, it's attached to the ship. If the ship doesn't swim down the page, neither does the anchor (oh no this analogy is not going well) but if the ship DOES swim down the page, so does the anchor. Wow, that was a informative.
